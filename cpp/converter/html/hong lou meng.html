  • 「假作真時 真亦假，無為有處 有還無。」
    • ( jia zuo zhen shi zhen yi jia, wu wei you chu you huan [hai????] wu)
    • "truth becomes fiction when the fiction's true; / real becomes not-real when the unreal's real." (translated by david hawkes)
    • “when the unreal is taken for the real, then the real becomes unreal; where non-existence is taken for existence, then existence becomes non-existence.” (translated by chi-chen wang)
    • “when falsehood disguises itself as the truth, the truth is also a lie; there exists a place for nothingness when existence returns nothing.” (translated by myself)

    And from ch 116 —

    • 「假去真來 真勝假，無原有是 有非無。」
      • ( jia qu zhen lai zhen sheng jia , wu yuan you shi you fei wu )
      • "when Fiction departs and Truth appears, / Truth prevails; / though Not-real was once Real, the Real is never unreal." (translated by john minford)
      • “when lies are gone and truth comes in, truth has defeated the lie; while nothingness was originally something, something is inherently un-nothing.” (translated by myself)
      • i pretty much interpret these couplets as being about sleeping and being in a dream
      • when you fall asleep and dream, you’re typically not aware that you are dreaming; hence, something unreal is taken for reality
      • however, if we’re not dreaming, it feels as if we don’t exist at all. your entire existence becomes “unaware of existence” and functionally, you just don’t exist. are we dead or are we dreaming? who’s to say.
      • in the second couplet, your dream fades and you wake up to reality, showing the waking world to be your true reality, thus defeating any question of which version you really are (in ref to zhuang zhou’s butterfly dream), and while your dreamless body doesn’t feel like it exists, you are never truly gone, as it is still there in the real world
      • our dreams reflect the waking world; they cannot exist w/o first having experienced smth real
      • in the context of the fic, “that which is real was never unreal” → though it was in a dream (unreality), the love that blossomed while they were asleep was still real and still exists in the waking world
      • 樊房更比碧天沈， (fan2 fang2 geng4 bi3 bi4 tian1 shen3,)
        春不常留恨不禁。 (chun1 bu4 chang2 liu2 hen4 bu jin4.)

        • in a cage even more remote than the blue of the sky
          your youth will not last long, you cannot help regretting this
          you have learned that a beauty does not have a bad fate,
          but in this life you have missed out on girlish innocence
      • zhou qi poem abt yuanchun in hong lou meng t/l by ellen widmer in the beauty and the book
      • “a beauty does not have a bad fate” references the common chinese phrase “a beauty has a bad fate”
      • the accompanying illustration does not show yuanchun’s face, suggesting her role as imperial consort has deprived her of her identity as well as her girlish innocence
      • the word for cage used in the poem implies a birdcage

      • “There’s nothing in it really,” said Daiyu. “There’s really hardly anything to learn. In Regulated Verse there are always four couplets: the “opening couplet,” the “developing couplet,” the “turning couplet,” and the “concluding couplet.” In the two middle couplets, the “developing” and “turning” ones, you have to have tone-contrast and parallelism. That’s to say in each of these couplets the even tones of one line have to contrast with the oblique tones in the other and vice versa. and the substantives and non-substantives have to balance each other, though if you’ve got a really good, original line it doesn’t matter all that much even if the tone-contrast and parallelism are all wrong.”
        • on good poetry, from ch. 48, translated by fawkes
      • “You’ve hit it exactly,” said Daiyu. “As a matter of fact, even the language is not of primary importance. The really important things are the ideas that lie behind it.
